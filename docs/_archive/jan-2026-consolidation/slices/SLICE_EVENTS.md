## The Upstream Problem

Why do events exist at all?

**Humans need shared moments to bond.**

Not shared spaces. Not shared chats. **Shared moments in time.**

You can be in the same group chat for months and feel nothing. You can attend ONE event with someone and feel like you know them.

**Synchronized attention creates bonds that asynchronous communication cannot.**

Being in a Discord server = ambient co-location. You're technically "together" but not really.

Being at an event = synchronized attention. For this hour, we're all HERE, doing THIS, together.

---

## The Deeper Psychology

What happens at an event that doesn't happen in chat?

### 1. Commitment Was Made
You RSVPed. You showed up. That's not nothing.

Commitment creates psychological investment. "I chose to be here." That choice becomes part of your identity.

**Chat requires no commitment. Events require commitment. Commitment creates value.**

### 2. Bodies Were Present
Even virtual events have more presence than chat. Physical events? You're actually THERE. Same air. Same room. Same moment.

**Bodies create bonds that text cannot.** You remember where you were. You remember who you sat next to.

### 3. Time Was Bounded
An event has a beginning and an end. It's a contained experience.

Chat goes on forever. There's no "we experienced this together."

**Bounded time creates shared memory.** "Remember that event?" vs "Remember that conversation?"

### 4. Stakes Were Real
If an event flops, everyone knows. If it's great, everyone knows.

Chat is low-stakes. Delete your message. Edit it. No one noticed.

**Events have consequence. Consequence creates meaning.**

---

## The One Job

**An event's job is to create a shared moment that becomes a shared memory.**

Not "gather people." Not "fill a calendar." Not "drive engagement."

**Create moments that people remember together.**

The best events become stories. "Remember when..." That's the job.

---

## Why Events Are The Strongest Discovery Path

### Lower Commitment Than Joining
Joining a space = identity claim. "I'm the kind of person who's in this group."

RSVPing to an event = curiosity. "This sounds interesting." Way lower stakes.

**Events let you try a community without committing to it.**

### Concrete Value Proposition
Space: "Join our pre-med society."
Event: "MCAT study session, Thursday 7pm, free pizza."

The event has specificity. Time. Place. Value.

**Events answer "what will I actually get?" in ways spaces can't.**

### Social Proof Is Visible
Space: "47 members" — what does that mean?

Event: "Sarah, Jake, and 12 others going" — real people committing.

**Event RSVP is stronger social proof than membership.**

### The Trojan Horse
You go to the event. It's great. You meet people.

"Oh, this was hosted by Pre-Med Society? Maybe I should join."

**The event sells the space. The space didn't have to sell itself.**

## The Anticipation Engine

Most platforms are reactive. Something happened, you see it, you respond.

**Events are anticipatory. Something is COMING.**

### The Forward Pull
"In 2 days: Photography walk"
"Tomorrow: Study session"
"In 3 hours: Guest speaker"

You check back not because of notifications — because you're anticipating.

**Anticipation creates pull without push.**

### The Countdown Effect
As an event approaches, energy builds:
- 1 week out: "Oh that sounds cool"
- 3 days out: "I should RSVP"
- 1 day out: "That's tomorrow!"
- 3 hours out: "Almost time"
- Live: "I'm here"

**The countdown creates momentum.** Each stage is a touchpoint without being annoying.

### The Social Acceleration
"Sarah just RSVPed"
"15 people going — up from 10 yesterday"
"Trending event this week"

You see momentum. Others are committing.

**FOMO that's earned, not manufactured.**

## The RSVP Mechanics

RSVP isn't a button. **RSVP is a commitment mechanism.**

### The Spectrum of Intention

| Status | Meaning | Psychology |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Interested | "Might go" | Low commitment, testing |
| Going | "I'll be there" | Real commitment |
| Maybe | "Depends" | Hedging, but engaged |
| Can't go | "Not this time" | Still engaged with event |

**Each status is a relationship with the event, not just a data point.**

### The Commitment Escalation
Interested → Going is a micro-conversion.

"You marked interested. 3 friends are now going. Still thinking about it?"

**Gentle escalation from curiosity to commitment.**

### The Show-Up Rate
RSVP ≠ Attendance. The gap matters.

- High show rate = healthy event culture
- Low show rate = RSVPs mean nothing

**The platform should track and surface show rates.** Events with high show rates are more valuable.

## The Post-Event Moment

**Most platforms ignore what happens after.** But post-event is where bonds solidify.

### The Shared Memory
Right after an event ends:

> **Photography Walk recap:**
> - 18 attended
> - Photos shared: 47
> - "People you connected with": Jake, Maria
> - Best moments compilation

The event becomes a memory artifact.

### The Connection Prompt
"You attended with Jake. Want to connect?"

**Events are connection factories.** The platform should harvest those connections.

### The Follow-Up Engine
For recurring events:
"Last MCAT study session had 23 people. Want to schedule the next one?"

For one-offs:
"Your photography walk was a hit. 18 people, 4.8 rating. Host another?"

**The platform helps successful events repeat.**

### The Feedback Loop
Quick post-event:
- "How was it?" (1-5 stars, one tap)
- "What was best?" (optional)
- "Who made it great?" (tag people)

This feeds into recommendations, space health, and social graph.

## Structural Classification

**Events are STRUCTURAL because they create increasing returns over time.**

### Mechanism 1: Preference Lock-In
Every RSVP teaches the platform what you like. Recommendations improve. Leaving means losing that learned preference model.

**Your event taste is non-portable.**

### Mechanism 2: Social Integration
Events create connections. Connections create reasons to attend more events. Your social life becomes integrated with the platform.

**Your event-based social graph is non-portable.**

### Mechanism 3: Memory Accumulation
Events become memories. Memories become identity. "I'm someone who goes to photography walks."

**Your event history is non-portable.**

### Mechanism 4: Network Density Effects
More people using events → more social proof → more RSVPs → better events → more people.

**The events get better because more people use them.**

## Leader vs Member Experience

### Leader Experience
**Needs:**
- Fast creation (AI-assisted)
- Beautiful output (auto-generated cards)
- Visibility (who's coming, analytics)
- Amplification (easy sharing, space distribution)
- Recognition (host reputation)

**First action:** Create event in 10 seconds

**Win state:** "My events always fill up now"

### Member Experience
**Needs:**
- Discovery (what's happening)
- Social proof (who's going)
- Low-risk commitment (interested → going)
- Anticipation (countdown, reminders)
- Memory (post-event artifacts)

**First action:** Browse and RSVP

**Win state:** "I never miss what matters anymore"

## Design Principles

### 1. Anticipation > Notification
Create forward pull, not backward push. Countdowns, not pings.

### 2. Social proof is primary
"Who's going" matters more than event description.

### 3. RSVP is commitment
Treat it seriously. Track show rates. Reward reliability.

### 4. Post-event matters
The event ends but the memory persists. Harvest connections.

### 5. Import is legitimate
External events are first-class. Comprehensiveness wins.

### 6. Creation should be instant
One sentence → event. AI does the work.

### 7. Share cards are the product
Beautiful, shareable, self-marketing.

### 8. Events sell spaces
The Trojan horse. Event discovery → space discovery.

## The Moat

Events are structural. The moat is:

- **Memory archive** — Events you've experienced

**A competitor can copy features. They cannot copy your event history, your social integration, or your learned preferences.**
